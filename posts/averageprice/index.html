What is an average price?

August 05, 2021

Suppose that a petrol station has sold 1,642,500L of petrol over the year, and counts $2,217,375 in revenues. That’s an average price of $1.35 per L of petrol.

$$ \cfrac{1,642,500}{2,217,375} = 1.35 $$

Now suppose that you are a car owner. Every day, you drive past the local petrol station and note down the advertised price. 60% of these days, the price is the typical $1.30 per L. For 10% of these days, it’s significantly cheaper at $1.20. And on the remaining 30% of the days, petrol is at a temporary shortage, so prices are hiked to $1.40. You calculate an average price of $1.32 per L.

$$1.20 * 0.1 + 1.30 * 0.6 + 1.40 * 0.3 = 1.32$$

There are two different averages for the price of petrol, but this is no contradiction. There is a different price advertised each day, and this difference can be achieved if more petrol is being bought on the more expensive days.

There is a weird interaction between price, time and volume here. Here’s another example. If a store makes no sales year-round, but then has a flurry of customers rushing through the door during the 50% off store-wide Christmas sale, what should be the average price?

Trading Price vs. Trading Volume

Price is only an advertising mechanism. Volume is where you find the true market value of goods. It’s an interesting and underappreciated idea, and it has severe implications on decision making. If it didn’t, then day-traders wouldn’t be using these ideas.
